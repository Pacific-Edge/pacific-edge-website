# Brand Voice — Pacific Edge AI

Applies to every word on the website: headlines, subheads, section labels, body copy, CTAs, testimonials framing, FAQ answers. This is the standard, not a suggestion — see the enforcement block in `CLAUDE.md`.

## The one-line rule

Write like you're describing the product to a smart, skeptical customer who will notice every exaggeration. Literal, grounded, concise, confident. No slogans, no wordplay, no comparisons, no superlatives.

## Reference bar

Stripe, Snowflake, Cloudflare, AWS, serious YC-startup copy. Confidence comes from precision, not adjectives.

## What "grounded" means in practice

- **Literal and specific.** Say what the product does in the plainest accurate words. A concrete claim ("texts your waitlist and fills the cancelled slot") beats a slogan every time.
- **Confidence through restraint.** Make one plain claim and stop. Don't reach for intensifiers to sound bigger.
- **Declarative, not comparative.** State what you do. Let the reader draw the contrast — don't draw it for them.
- **Respect the reader.** Assume a competent professional who runs a real business. No pain-mirroring, no talking down to their current workflow.
- **Only claim what's true today.** If a feature is manual, in progress, or a heuristic stub, the copy must not imply it's automatic, complete, or AI-magic. Grounded claims are also accurate claims — this matters most for a company handling client data.
- **Less is more.** Concise beats exhaustive. A subtitle can be one sentence. Bullet points beat a paragraph. More words is not more information — cut until every sentence is load-bearing.

## Banned patterns

| Pattern | Why it fails | Example from this site |
|---|---|---|
| Anthropomorphized folksiness | Gives the product a cute personality; cute reads as small | "How Janice earns her keep" |
| Slogan syntax (antithesis/parallelism) | Built for rhythm, not information; the second half always over-claims | "One hire, endless shifts," "One Janice, every channel" |
| Defensive/comparative positioning | Defining yourself against a lesser category concedes you're in it | "Not your average bot" |
| Pain-mirroring that belittles the reader | Dramatizes the customer's workflow as pathetic to make the product look heroic; condescends | "Sounds like your week... it lives in 10 spreadsheets" |
| Absolute/superlative over-claims | Unprovable, and the reader knows it | "endless," "every channel," "seamless," "effortless," "powerful," "revolutionary" |
| Cutesy section labels | The label performs instead of informs | "Three ways to put Janice to work" instead of "Pricing" |
| Fragment-punchiness / second-read wink copy | Short jabby lines engineered to sound clever; the house style of AI marketing copy | — |

## Before / after

| Before | After | Why |
|---|---|---|
| "How Janice earns her keep" | "What Janice does" | Literal section label, no character voice |
| "One hire, endless shifts" | "One AI employee covers your front desk around the clock" | States the actual claim, drops the slogan template and the absolute |
| "Not your average bot" | (delete — state the capability instead) | Comparative positioning adds no information |
| "One Janice, every channel" | "Janice answers calls, texts, and web chat from one system" | Specific channels named, no "every" over-claim |
| "Sounds like your week... it lives in 10 spreadsheets" | "If your booking process spans a call, a text thread, and a spreadsheet, Janice consolidates it into one." | States the situation neutrally, no belittling framing |
| "Three ways to put Janice to work" | "Pricing" | Section labels are literal |
| "Trustworthy, privacy-focused service" | (keep — already grounded) | Literal, no adjective inflation |

## Section labels

Name sections for what they are. "Pricing," "How it works," "Security," "Industries we serve," "FAQ." Boring is trustworthy. This site's existing landing-page titles ("How we grow your business," "Industries we serve," "Simple process, powerful results," "Trustworthy, privacy-focused service") are the bar — match that register everywhere, including sub-pages.

## What to keep

- The Janice name and persona as a product identity (the AI employee is called Janice) — this is not banned. What's banned is writing copy *from* Janice's voice as a cutesy character, or building slogans around her name.
- Emoji in nav/mock UI (existing design-system convention, unrelated to prose voice).
- Concrete stats, testimonials, and sourced claims — keep these, they're the opposite of the problem.
